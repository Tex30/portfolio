Salifort Motors HR Analytics

Employee retention prediction model using machine learning to identify factors influencing turnover

Completed: March 2025
Machine Learning, Python, HR Analytics
  • Overview
  • Process
  • Results
  • Conclusion

Project Overview

A mid-sized company was experiencing high employee turnover rates, resulting in significant recruitment costs, reduced productivity, and loss of institutional knowledge. HR leaders needed to understand the factors driving departures and identify at-risk employees before they resigned to implement proactive retention strategies.

Client

Salifort Motors HR Department

Duration

2 weeks

Tools Used

Python, Scikit-learn, Pandas, Matplotlib, Seaborn

Team Size

3 data scientists

Business Problem

Salifort Motors was experiencing high turnover rates that were affecting their business in multiple ways:

  • High turnover costs (1.5-2x annual salary per employee lost)
  • Decreased team productivity during transition periods
  • Lost institutional knowledge and expertise
  • Reduced morale among remaining team members
  • Limited visibility into why employees were leaving

Dataset Description

The dataset contained 15,000 employee records with 10 features including:

  • Demographics (age, gender)
  • Job characteristics (department, role, salary)
  • Work experience (tenure, previous companies)
  • Performance metrics (satisfaction scores, evaluation results)
  • Work arrangements (hours worked, remote work percentage)

Technical Approach

Data Preparation & Exploration

  • Cleaned and preprocessed HR dataset containing 14,999 employee records
  • Engineered relevant features including satisfaction metrics, workload indicators, and tenure variables
  • Conducted exploratory data analysis to identify preliminary patterns
  • Visualized correlations between key variables and turnover outcomes
  • Applied appropriate transformations to optimize model performance

Model Development

  • Implemented multiple classification algorithms (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost)
  • Performed hyperparameter tuning using RandomizedSearchCV and GridSearchCV
  • Optimized decision threshold to balance precision and recall
  • Validated model performance using stratified cross-validation
  • Ensured model robustness through rigorous testing on holdout data

Evaluation & Interpretation

  • Evaluated model using comprehensive metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, F1, AUC)
  • Generated feature importance rankings to identify key turnover drivers
  • Created visualizations including confusion matrices, ROC curves, and precision-recall curves
  • Analyzed patterns in misclassified cases to understand model limitations
  • Developed interpretable insights for non-technical stakeholders

Results & Impact

The XGBoost model demonstrated exceptional performance in predicting employee turnover:

  • Achieved 98.5% prediction accuracy with balanced precision (98.1%) and recall (93.0%)
  • Identified workload (number of projects), satisfaction level, and tenure as the three primary drivers of employee turnover
  • Discovered department-specific turnover patterns requiring targeted interventions
  • Developed an early warning system capable of identifying 93% of employees at risk of leaving
  • Created data-driven recommendations that could potentially reduce turnover costs by $1.2M annually

Key Predictors of Turnover

The model identified the following as the top predictors of employee turnover:

  1. Satisfaction level (29% importance) - Employees with satisfaction scores below 0.4 were 4.5x more likely to leave
  2. Weekly hours (18% importance) - Working over 50 hours/week increased turnover probability by 270%
  3. Number of projects (14% importance) - Both too few (1-2) and too many (6+) projects correlated with higher turnover
  4. Time since last promotion (12% importance) - Employees not promoted in 4+ years showed 3.2x higher turnover
  5. Salary level (11% importance) - Low-salary employees had 2.8x higher turnover than high-salary counterparts

Identified Employee Segments

Cluster analysis revealed four distinct employee segments at risk:

  • Overworked high-performers (25% of departures) - High project load, long hours, high evaluations
  • Undervalued veterans (22% of departures) - Long tenure, few promotions, stagnant salary growth
  • Disengaged newcomers (18% of departures) - Low satisfaction, less than 2 years at company
  • Career-blocked specialists (16% of departures) - Medium tenure, high skill, limited advancement opportunities

Business Recommendations

Based on the model findings, I developed five strategic recommendations to address turnover:

  • Workload Management Program

    Establish project allocation guidelines with maximum thresholds per employee, with a recommended limit of 3-4 active projects. Implement a workload monitoring system that alerts managers when employees exceed optimal capacity.

  • Satisfaction Enhancement Initiative

    Implement quarterly pulse surveys with concrete follow-up actions, focusing on the key satisfaction drivers identified in the analysis. Create a formal feedback loop with mandatory action plans for departments with below-average satisfaction scores.

  • Tenure-Based Retention Strategy

    Design targeted programs for employees at critical tenure milestones (2-3 years, 4-5 years), including career development conversations, skill expansion opportunities, and potential role adjustments to maintain engagement.

  • Performance Evaluation Reform

    Revise review processes to focus on development rather than assessment, with an emphasis on growth opportunities and skill acquisition. Separate compensation discussions from performance feedback to create a more positive evaluation experience.

  • Work-Life Balance Improvement

    Introduce flexible scheduling options across all departments and actively monitor overtime hours. Create a culture where taking personal time is encouraged, not penalized, with managers modeling appropriate boundaries.

Implementation Plan

To effectively implement these recommendations, I proposed the following action plan:

  1. Deploy Early Warning System (1-2 months): Integrate the predictive model into HR systems to identify at-risk employees and enable proactive intervention.
  2. Pilot Targeted Interventions (2-3 months): Select high-risk departments for initial implementation of retention strategies, focusing on quick wins and measurable outcomes.
  3. Scale Successful Programs (3-6 months): Evaluate pilot results and expand successful initiatives company-wide, with customizations based on departmental needs.
  4. Enhance Data Collection (Ongoing): Identify additional data points to improve model accuracy, including exit interview insights and employee engagement metrics.
  5. Measure Financial Impact (6-12 months): Calculate cost savings and ROI of retention initiatives, tracking key metrics including turnover rate, replacement costs, and productivity impacts.

Reflections & Lessons Learned

This project reinforced the importance of careful data preparation and feature engineering in building effective predictive models. The most valuable insight was learning to balance technical sophistication with business interpretability - the most complex model isn't always the most useful if stakeholders can't understand how to act on its outputs.

Key lessons learned:

  • Business context is critical: Understanding the true cost of turnover helped frame the problem and quantify the value of solutions
  • Model explainability matters: Stakeholders needed to understand why employees were leaving, not just who might leave
  • Cross-functional collaboration: Working closely with HR experts improved feature selection and recommendation development
  • Threshold optimization: Finding the right balance between precision and recall was essential for practical application
  • Continuous improvement: Designing a feedback loop for model refinement as more data becomes available
